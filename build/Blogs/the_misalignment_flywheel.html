The Misalignment Flywheel

The Misalignment Flywheel — Every company begins with a grand vision — a tomorrow that's better than today. This vision acts as a powerful attractor, drawing in passionate engineers, innovative designers, and ambitious product professionals who yearn to make their mark on the world.

In the beginning, the alignment is beautiful. Founders and leaders harness this collective talent by showing each individual how they fit into the grand picture, empowering them to bring their fullest repertoire of talent and experience to the table.

But then, something shifts.

The Misalignment Flywheel Starts to Turn

As the initial excitement of employee orientation fades and teams collide with market realities, something starts to shift. The carefully crafted plans begin to crack under pressure:

  1. Customer demands scream for urgent attention
  2. Stakeholder "hunches" redirect resources
  3. Reactive decision-making hijacks the product roadmap

As product-market fit proves more elusive than expected, companies enter a perilous phase where momentum begins building in the wrong direction. What follows is a pattern so consistent it behaves like a flywheel — each turn building momentum that makes the next turn even more likely.

The Cycle Begins

Here's how the flywheel starts spinning:

  1. Stage 1: Engineering Doubt Emerges — Engineering talent, once passionate about the mission, starts questioning whether their work is having the promised impact.
  2. Stage 2: Management Turns Defensive — Product and middle management interpret these questions as doubt in their direction and push back.
  3. Stage 3: Questions Intensify — Unsatisfied, engineering inquiries persist, now backed by evidence.
  4. Stage 4: Frustration Mounts — Product and middle management, squeezed between team unrest and lack of market success, double down.
  5. Stage 5: Confrontation Erupts — Engineers become more assertive, demanding to understand why specific features are prioritized.
  6. Stage 6: Leadership Clamps Down — Executives essentially tell engineering to "shut up and code."
  7. Stage 7: The Silent Exodus — Disheartened engineers start quietly updating their LinkedIn profiles.
  8. Stage 8: Deceleration Sets In — As talent bleeds out, product-market fit becomes even more elusive.
  9. Stage 9: Control Overtakes Culture — Overwhelmed managers tighten their grip, stifling innovation.
The misalignment flywheel isn’t just a cautionary tale — it’s a very real mechanical process that affects both startups and established companies.

Like any flywheel, it builds momentum gradually, but unlike productive flywheels that drive growth, this one compounds organizational friction.

Breaking the Cycle

The good news? Understanding it as a flywheel gives us clear points of intervention. In Part 2 of this series, we’ll explore:

  1. The mechanics behind why this flywheel is so powerful
  2. Early warning signs that your flywheel is beginning to spin
  3. Specific interventions to slow and reverse the momentum
  4. Preventive measures to maintain healthy alignment

Because here’s the truth: while the misalignment flywheel can feel unstoppable once it builds momentum, it’s not inevitable. Companies can intervene at any point — but the sooner, the better.
